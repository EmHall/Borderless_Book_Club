Russia
The roots of Russian literature can be traced to the Middle Ages, but the golden age arrived in the 1830s. Alexander Pushkin was central to this movement. The first great Russian novelist was Nikolai Gogol, then came Ivan Turgenev, then Leo Tolstoy and then Fyodor Dostoevsky. In the second half of the century Anton Chekhov excelled in short stories and plays. The beginning of the 20th century is known as the Silver Age of Russian poetry, among whom were Alexander Blok, Anna Akhmatova, Sergei Yesenin and Boris Pasternak, all of whom suffered under the dawning of the Soviet era.
After 1917 Revolution, Russian literature split into Soviet and white émigré parts. In the 1930s Socialist realism became the predominant trend, and its leading figure was Maxim Gorky. Other writers such as short story Nobel Prize-winning writer Ivan Bunin, continued to write in exile. Nobel Prize-winning novelist Aleksandr Solzhenitsyn challenged the Soviet system. The Khrushchev Thaw revitalised literature but prosecutions continuted nonetheless.
The end of the 20th century was a difficult period for Russian literature, with few distinct voices. Among the most discussed authors of this period were Victor Pelevin, who gained popularity with short stories and novels, novelist and playwright Vladimir Sorokin, and the poet Dmitri Prigov. In the 21st century, a new generation of Russian authors appeared, differing greatly from the postmodernist Russian prose of the late 20th century, which lead critics to speak about "new realism".
